Methodology and reader's guide

The 60-second tour

This product answers: what changed, who is exposed, how bad it gets under stress, what breached, and what to do next. Suggested path: start on Cockpit (top movers, priority risks, breaches), switch persona to reframe it, click any risk for the drill-down drawer, then open the Stress lab to combine shocks and edit pass-through assumptions.

What each tab does
  • Cockpit — the workbench: top movers, composite-scored priority risks, exposure map, concentration, counterparty and breach queues, and the output center. Reorders by persona.
  • Who is exposed — every company's full brief: composite risk scores, commodity dependencies, products, weaknesses, and policy impacts.
  • Company deep dive — one company end to end: where every input physically comes from, product-by-product vulnerability, and its sensitivity to every shock.
  • Stress lab — stack real or custom shocks in either direction, edit sector pass-through / hedge / inventory assumptions, and rank company margin impact by band.
  • Commodity map — the relationship map: what is made from what, whose prices move together, and which countries control supply.
  • Advisory pipeline — the resulting engagement opportunities with deadlines and a pursuit tracker.
  • Deadlines — dated regulatory obligations with live countdowns.
  • Risk landscape / Evidence log — the analytical quadrant, and the raw weekly research reports with every source cited.
How to read the colors and shapes
  • Red / amber / green nodes and squares — supply concentration: red means one country or a handful of suppliers controls the market (cobalt-style chokepoint); green means supply is spread out.
  • Badges on companies — "overall" is the blended six-lens risk grade; "policy" is how hard current regulation hits them (urgent / material / watch).
  • Red bars vs green bars in scenarios — red = input costs rise; green = the company produces that commodity and gains when its price spikes.
  • Solid arrows on the map — physical transformation ("becomes"): crude oil → diesel. Dashed blue — prices move together. Dotted amber — same country controls supply of both.
  • Blue vs coral cells in the correlation matrix — blue = prices historically move together; coral = they move opposite; the number is the strength (−1 to +1).
Glossary — the technical terms, in plain English
Money and risk basics
  • Commodity — a raw or semi-processed material bought in bulk where one supplier's tonne is like another's: crude oil, steel, cocoa, gold.
  • Exposure — how much money is at stake when a price moves. If palm oil is 12% of your costs and it rises 50%, you lose ~6% of your cost base.
  • Cost base / input costs — everything a company spends to make its products. "Steel = 14% of cost base" means 14 rupees of every 100 spent go to steel.
  • Volatility — how violently a price swings. Cocoa tripling in 2024 = extreme volatility; milk prices barely moving = low.
  • Supply concentration — how few places supply the world. DRC mines 73% of cobalt: one political crisis can reprice the whole market.
  • Import dependence — the share of India's consumption bought abroad. High import share means a weaker rupee directly raises costs even if the world price is flat.
  • Chokepoint — a single origin, plant, or route whose failure breaks the whole chain (Ukraine made ~half the world's chip-grade neon before 2022).
  • Pass-through — how much of a cost rise travels to the next buyer. Our model assumes 50% survives each processing step: crude +40% → diesel +20%.
  • Correlation (ρ) — a number from −1 to +1 measuring whether two prices move together. +0.8 = nearly always together; 0 = unrelated; −0.5 = tend to move opposite.
  • Net beneficiary — a company that produces a commodity gains when its price spikes. ONGC pumps oil, so an oil shock helps it — that's the green bar.
  • Windfall levy — an extra tax governments impose on producers when prices spike, capping the benefit.
Treasury and hedging terms
  • Treasury — the corporate function that manages cash, debt, currencies, and financial risk.
  • Hedging — paying a little today to lock a price for tomorrow; insurance against price and currency moves.
  • Forward contract — an agreement to exchange currency at a fixed rate on a future date; the workhorse of Indian corporate FX hedging.
  • Cancel-and-rebook — the old habit of cancelling a forward and re-striking it when the market moved your way. The RBI barred this on 1 April 2026 (A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 03) — which is why every corporate hedge policy needs a rewrite.
  • NDF (non-deliverable forward) — an offshore bet on the rupee settled in dollars, without actually exchanging rupees. The RBI barred banks from offering these to residents and non-residents on 1 April 2026, pushing hedging onshore.
  • FCNR(B) deposit — a foreign-currency account NRIs hold at Indian banks. From 8 June 2026 the RBI absorbs banks' full hedging cost (~280–300 bp) on fresh 3–5 year deposits via a swap facility (deposit window closes 30 Sep 2026) to pull dollars into India and defend the rupee.
  • ECB (external commercial borrowing) — a foreign-currency loan taken by an Indian company; cheap until the rupee falls and repayment gets expensive.
  • UFCE (unhedged foreign currency exposure) — an RBI rule forcing banks to hold extra capital against borrowers who haven't hedged their FX debt — which makes unhedged clients expensive for banks.
  • EUA (EU emissions allowance) — the tradeable permit price of one tonne of CO2 in Europe; the price CBAM bills are calculated from, and a brand-new hedgeable risk for Indian exporters.
  • Natural hedge — when exposures cancel out on their own: ONGC earns dollar-linked revenue, so its dollar costs hurt less.
Policy guide — each regulation in one breath
CBAM — the EU's carbon border tax (live since 1 Jan 2026)

European factories pay for their CO2 emissions; CBAM makes importers pay the same, so foreign steel can't undercut European steel on carbon. Anyone selling steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizer, hydrogen, or electricity into the EU must count the emissions embedded in their goods and buy certificates priced off the EU carbon market. The liability started accruing 1 Jan 2026; certificates go on sale 1 Feb 2027; the first bill is due 30 Sep 2027. From 2027, importers must hold certificates covering 50% of embedded emissions each quarter (the Omnibus cut this from 80%), and 2027 purchases are priced off the 2026 quarterly-average ETS price. For Indian steel and aluminum exporters this is a new cost line and a new financial risk (the EUA price) their treasuries have never managed. Source: European Commission.

EUDR — the EU deforestation rule (applies 30 Dec 2026)

To sell cocoa, coffee, palm oil, rubber, soy, wood, or cattle products in the EU, you must prove — with GPS coordinates of the farm plots — that the land wasn't deforested after 2020. Applies to large companies from 30 Dec 2026 (postponed twice; smaller firms mid-2027). For Indian FMCG and paper exporters this means traceability systems and possibly switching suppliers. Source: Regulation (EU) 2025/2650.

EU–India FTA — the trade deal (concluded 27 Jan 2026, not yet in force)

The largest trade agreement either side has ever negotiated: tariffs dismantled both ways across a market of two billion people. Indian pharma, textiles, and auto components gain EU access; EU cars, wines, and machinery gain Indian access. It only takes effect after ratification (Council decision, EP consent, Indian Cabinet) — entry into force is expected in early 2027, with EU legal scrubbing concluding around July 2026. That timeline is the pitch window: companies whose euro revenue will grow are under-hedged today. Source: European Commission IP/26/184.

RBI 2026 rupee defense — April rules, June funding package

With the rupee sliding past 95/$, the RBI moved in two steps. 1 April 2026 — A.P. (DIR Series) Circular No. 03: banks barred from offering INR NDFs to residents or non-residents, rebooking of cancelled INR forwards banned, related-party INR derivatives barred. Every corporate forward-rolling playbook became non-compliant that day. 5–8 June 2026 — the funding package: the RBI absorbs banks' full hedging cost (~280–300 bp) on fresh 3–5 year FCNR(B) deposits mobilised to 30 Sep 2026 (USD deposit rates jumped to 5.5–7.1%), plus concessional currency swaps on state-owned companies' foreign loans. Net effect: hedge policies need re-papering now, and the funding arbitrage is time-boxed — the single biggest treasury-advisory trigger in this dashboard. Sources: RBI circular of 1 Apr 2026; RBI June 2026 package.

US Section 232 — metal tariffs (restructured Apr–Jun 2026)

US national-security tariffs on metals: 50% on goods that are almost entirely steel/aluminum/copper, 25% on metal-heavy derivatives, a temporary 15% on industrial equipment until end-2027, and exemption below 15% metal content. Keeps the US market expensive for Indian metal exporters — pushing volumes toward the EU, where CBAM is waiting. Source: White House proclamations, Apr and Jun 2026.

China export controls — the refining chokepoint

China dominates the processing (not necessarily mining) of many critical inputs — ~65–70% of lithium refining, ~90% of graphite, most rare earths, the majority of India's pharma raw materials (APIs). Since 2023 it has progressively required export licenses on several of these. For India this is a supply-security risk that no hedge fixes — only dual sourcing and inventories. Sources: IEA; Dept of Pharmaceuticals.

India's own levers — gold duty and the carbon market

India raised bullion import duty from 6% to 15% (10% basic customs duty + 5% AIDC, silver included) effective 13 May 2026, explicitly to slow dollar outflows and defend the rupee (gold imports hit a record $72B in FY26 — hitting jewellers like Titan directly). Separately, India's Carbon Credit Trading Scheme (CCTS) is phasing in a domestic carbon price that will eventually reach cement, steel, and power — CBAM logic, applied at home. Sources: CBIC/Commerce Ministry; Ministry of Power.

CommodityComputed: Σ (cost-base weight × 0.4 supply concentration + 0.4 India import share + 0.2 price volatility). Weights from annual reports where disclosed, otherwise labeled estimates; concentration and import shares from USGS, PPAC, IEA, USDA, ICCO, Ministry of Steel.
PolicyComputed from the latest weekly cycle's impact status (urgent 5, material 4, watch 3, none 1). Official documents only.

ConcentrationQualitative rating: product, customer, geography, or single-segment profit dependence per segment disclosures.
TransitionQualitative rating: decarbonization exposure, technology shifts, documented competitive disruption.
Data pipeline

A six-agent research cycle (planner → document scout → risk analyst → company mapper → rival monitor → service mapper) scans EUR-Lex, European Commission DGs, Consilium, RBI, DGFT, CBIC, PIB, WTO, USTR, Federal Register, and exchange filings. Reasoning is recorded before conclusions in each cycle JSON. Secondary sources are tagged and queued for primary retrieval. Cycle cadence is currently manual — cycle 2026-W28 (7 Jul 2026) is embedded; scheduled weekly automation ships with the live-data pipeline. Live market data: a daily GitHub Actions pipeline (scripts/pipeline.py, 18:30 IST) writes data/latest.json — ECB daily reference FX (frankfurter), EIA Brent/WTI/Henry Hub and IMF PCPS commodity indices (FRED, keyless), the LBMA gold PM fix, spot FX/gold (open.er-api.com / gold-api.com), and EUA plus the official CBAM certificate reference as dated manual marks. The page fetches it same-origin, with the embedded dated snapshot as offline fallback; an optional Alpha Vantage key remains as an analyst override for the correlation matrix.

Live data pipeline — computed statistics
  • Volatility — daily series (Brent, WTI, Henry Hub, LBMA gold, USD/INR): EWMA σ with λ = 0.94 (RiskMetrics), monthly = daily × √21. Monthly series (IMF PCPS): rolling 36-month std of monthly log returns. Feeds the Monte-Carlo model's σ (clamped 2–25%/month) where a real series exists; ratings remain the fallback for steel, coking coal, APIs, TiO2 and lithium, which have no free series.
  • Correlations — Pearson on up to 60 overlapping monthly log returns (minimum 24) across ~15 series; drives the commodity-map edges and the copula model's dependence matrix.
  • Regime — 0.5 × average 5-year vol percentile (Brent, Henry Hub, gold, USD/INR) + 0.3 × INR 20-day momentum (+3% = max) + 0.2 × breadth (share of series moving >5%); thresholds 45 / 60 / 75 → elevated / stressed / crisis.
  • Velocity — |20-day move|, capped (commodities 15%, FX 4%), where a daily series exists (the oil, gold and USD/INR risks); other risks keep curated velocity and are scored accordingly. Exposure and concentration remain curated in P1 — computing them from cost-base data is a P2 item.
  • Score history — each pipeline run appends every risk's composite score to data/history.json; sparklines and "what moved" read this real history (first point 17 Jul 2026).
  • ₹ figures — modelled impact % × FY26 disclosed cost base (revenue − EBITDA) from data/financials.json; each entry carries its source and as-of date. Verified at entry — re-verify before client use.
Caveats
  • Company cost-share weights are estimates where issuers do not disclose input-cost breakdowns; anchored figures cite their filing.
  • Qualitative lens scores are structured judgments from public facts — a prioritization layer, not measurements.
  • Scenario mathematics assume 50% cost pass-through per processing step, no hedging or inventories — structural exposure, not price forecasts.
